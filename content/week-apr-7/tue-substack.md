# Tuesday Substack (Free) — Apr 8

## Title: 5 Things That Actually Matter in AI This Week

Five stories from the past week that I think are worth your attention. Not a news recap — my take on what each one means for people who actually build things.

---

### 1. Anthropic went from $1B to $19B ARR in 14 months

Lenny Rachitsky just published an interview with Anthropic's Head of Growth, Amol Avasare. The numbers are absurd: $1 billion to $19 billion in annual recurring revenue in just over a year. That makes Claude the fastest-growing AI product in history.

But the interesting part isn't the number. It's how they did it.

They built an internal tool called CASH that lets Claude autonomously run growth experiments. Think about that — they're using their own AI to grow their own AI. And they index 70/30 toward big bets, which is the opposite of how most growth teams operate.

My take: If Anthropic's own growth team trusts AI to run experiments autonomously, the "AI can't do real work" argument is dead. The question isn't whether AI can do your job. It's whether you're using it to do your job better before someone else does.

---

### 2. Stripe's AI "Minions" now ship 1,300 PRs per week

A Stripe engineer went on ChatPRD's "How I AI" show and casually mentioned that their internal AI agents are landing 1,300 pull requests a week. No human coding. Just human review.

The kicker: engineers start their work from Slack, not a code editor. They react with an emoji and the agent does the rest.

My take: This is what "AI-native engineering" actually looks like. Not "AI assists the developer." AI IS the developer. The human is the reviewer, the PM, the quality gate. If you're still thinking about AI as an autocomplete tool, you're two years behind Stripe.

---

### 3. Simon Willison says mid-career engineers are more at risk than juniors

On Lenny's podcast, Simon Willison (creator of Django, one of the most respected voices in dev tooling) said something that made me uncomfortable: mid-career engineers are more at risk from AI than juniors.

His reasoning: juniors are learning with AI from day one. They're native. Mid-career engineers have habits and workflows built around manually writing code. They're the ones who resist the tools and fall behind.

My take: I've seen this in myself. My instinct is still to open a file and start typing. But the people shipping fastest right now are the ones who describe what they want and let the agent build it. The skill is shifting from "writing good code" to "directing good code." If you've been coding for 10 years, that transition is harder than you think.

---

### 4. Google released Gemma 4 under Apache 2.0

Google's new Gemma 4 model dropped this week with one major change: it's licensed under Apache 2.0. Previous versions had a custom license that restricted commercial use in annoying ways. This one is genuinely open.

My take: This matters more than the benchmarks. Apache 2.0 means you can build commercial products on Gemma without asking Google's permission. For solo builders and small teams, this is a game-changer. You no longer need to choose between "good model" and "actually ownable." I'm already planning to test it for a side project where I don't want vendor lock-in.

---

### 5. LinkedIn's editor ships iOS apps with a "dueling agent" setup

Daniel Roth — literally the editor of LinkedIn — builds production iOS apps in his spare time using Claude Code. His setup: two agents named "Bob the Builder" and "Ray the Reviewer." Bob builds. Ray reviews and pushes back. Daniel acts as the tiebreaker.

My take: This is almost identical to my own setup (Bolt + Hawk). The pattern is converging: builder agent + reviewer agent + human manager. If LinkedIn's own editor — a writer, not an engineer — can ship real apps this way, the barrier to building software has effectively collapsed. The people who win from here are the ones with good ideas and the patience to direct AI, not the ones with CS degrees.

---

That's the week. The through-line I see: we're past the "will AI work?" phase. We're deep into the "who adapts fastest?" phase.

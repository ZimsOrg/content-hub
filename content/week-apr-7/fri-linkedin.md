# Friday LinkedIn — Apr 11

## Title: Simon Willison writes 95% of his code from his phone. That sentence should scare mid-career engineers.

Simon Willison — creator of Django, one of the most respected developers alive — recently shared that he writes 95% of his code from his phone.

Not pseudocode. Not notes. Actual shipping code. From his phone.

He describes what he wants. The AI agent builds it. He reviews and ships.

But here's the part that made me uncomfortable:

He thinks mid-career engineers are MORE at risk from AI than juniors.

His reasoning:

→ Juniors are learning with AI from day one. It's native to them. They don't know any other way.

→ Mid-career engineers (5-15 years experience) have deeply ingrained habits. They open a file, they type code, they debug by reading stack traces. These habits were advantages for 20 years. Now they're friction.

→ Senior architects who think in systems are fine. Their value was never in the typing.

The dangerous middle: experienced enough to be set in your ways, not senior enough that your value is purely strategic.

I felt this in myself. My first instinct when I want to build something is still to open VS Code and start a file. But the fastest path now is to describe what I want to an AI agent and let it scaffold the whole thing. Then I review, adjust, and ship.

The skill shift is real:

Old world: "Can you write clean, efficient code?"
New world: "Can you clearly describe what needs to be built, evaluate AI output, and catch what it misses?"

The second skill set is harder to learn if you've spent a decade building the first one.

Three things I'm doing about it:

1. Forcing myself to start every project by describing it in natural language before touching any code. Break the "open editor first" habit.

2. Using the builder-reviewer pattern (two agents, one builds, one critiques). This teaches me to evaluate code faster than write it.

3. Investing time in system design and architecture thinking. That's the last thing AI will replace.

Simon also mentioned something called the "dark factory" pattern — where nobody writes OR reviews code. AI builds it. AI tests it. AI ships it. He thinks it's coming.

I don't know if I believe that yet. But I didn't believe I'd run a 6-agent team from my terminal either.

Are you adapting your workflow, or still coding the way you did two years ago? Honest answers only.

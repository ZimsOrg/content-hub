# LinkedIn Post — The Bottleneck Flipped

## Title: We can build faster than we can decide what's worth building. That's a new problem.

I led a team of four engineers who all coded with AI.

Each of them could get through 2 or 3 tickets a day. That's roughly 300 tickets a month across the team.

Nobody has 300 good product ideas a month. Nobody.

The first couple of months, you're fine. Everything is fresh. You can see all the obvious gaps, and there's a backlog of things that have been annoying users for months.

But by month three, you start feeling it.

You're sitting there trying to figure out what actually moves the needle versus what just keeps people occupied. And the moment you catch yourself writing tickets just to fill a sprint, you know something about the model is off.

At a place like Amazon, this problem is masked by scale. There's always another service to optimize, another metric to chase, another team's API to integrate. The work generates itself.

But at a company like Gusto — smaller, more focused, closer to the customer — you feel it faster. The surface area of valuable work is finite. And when your engineers can burn through it in weeks instead of quarters, you have to confront a question most engineering leaders have never faced:

What do we do when building is no longer the bottleneck?

For as long as I've been in engineering leadership, capacity was the constraint. You always had more ideas than your team could build. Prioritization frameworks existed because you had to choose — there was never enough engineering time to do everything.

AI flipped that. Quietly but fundamentally.

When your engineers can ship a feature or a fix in a couple of hours instead of a week, the constraint isn't their capacity anymore. It's whether you actually have enough valuable work to give them.

Here's what I've started doing differently:

→ Spending more time on discovery and less on delivery planning. The ratio used to be 20/80. Now it's closer to 50/50.

→ Encouraging engineers to spend time with customers directly. They need to understand the "why" behind the work, not just execute the "what."

→ Being honest when there isn't enough high-impact work for a sprint. A half-full sprint of meaningful work beats a packed sprint of filler.

→ Investing in quality and depth over quantity. If you can build 3x faster, build 1x and spend 2x on testing, polish, and architecture.

The teams that figure this out will ship better products. The ones that don't will ship more features that nobody asked for.

We can build faster than we can decide what's worth building. And I think most engineering leaders haven't run into that wall yet.

But they will.

Here's my honest take:

The engineering leader role is about to split in two. There will be leaders who manage output — shipping fast, hitting deadlines, keeping the machine running. And there will be leaders who manage insight — understanding customers deeply enough to always have the next meaningful problem ready.

The first type will be automated. The second type will be the most valuable people in the building.

If you're an engineering leader and your biggest skill is unblocking tickets and running standups, start worrying. If your biggest skill is knowing what's worth building before anyone else does, you've never been more valuable.

The future of engineering leadership isn't about shipping faster. It's about thinking better.
